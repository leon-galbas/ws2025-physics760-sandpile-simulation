The driven sandpile system is simulated by specifying an initial configuration $z_0(r)$ and iterating through the steps described in Algorithm \ref{alg:step}.

\begin{algorithm}
    \caption{Time evolution of a sandpile with a slow driving perturbation \cite{christensen1991dynamical}.}
    \label{alg:step}
    \KwIn{Initial sandpile state $z_0$, critical value $z_c$, number of macroscopic time steps $N_\mathrm{steps}$}
    \KwOut{Final state after $N_\mathrm{steps}$ steps}

    \For{$i=1$ \KwTo $N_\mathrm{steps}$}{
        \While{$\exists r\in R:\, z_\tau(r) > z_c$}{
            Apply relaxation $z_\tau(r) \to z_{\tau+1}(r)$ \;
        }
        Choose $r_0\in R$ randomly \;
        Apply perturbation at $r_0$ \;
    }
\end{algorithm}

In order to perform measurements of avalanche variables, every sandpile must be brought into a critical state through a burn-in phase first. Then measurements can be taken by subsequently perturbing the system and tracking desired variables.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{The burn-in phase} \label{sec:burn-in}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

While Christensen et al. initialize all sandpiles with $z_0=0$ everywhere and run Algorithm \ref{alg:step} until $\expval{z}(\tau)$ becomes stationary, we introduce two additional burn-in strategies, in an effort to reduce the burn-in time $T$ and converge to the stationary state more quickly.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Random initialization: For every $r\in R$ we initialize $z_0$ to an independent uniformly distributed integer between zero and $z_c$.
    \item Maximally stable initialization: We initialize $z_0(r) = z_c$ for all $r\in R$.
\end{itemize}
The idea behind these approaches is that instead of zero initialization, the sandpile already contains energy at the start. Therefore less perturbations are expected to be necessary to introduce energy and bring the empty system towards its critical state.

In order to implement the model burn-in, we need to define a criterion to decide whether a stationary state has been reached. We do this by logging $\expval{z}(\tau_k)$ every $\Delta\tau_c$ steps. After having obtained at least $W$ values, we do a linear least squares fit of $\expval{z}(\tau_k)$ against $k$ for a rolling window of the last $W$ values. If the slope $m$ of this fit satisfies $\abs{m}<\eps$ for some threshold $\eps > 0$, we say that $\expval{z}$ is stationary.

As mentioned in Section \ref{sec:critical-state}, the value of $\expval{z}(\tau)$ naturally fluctuates around $\overline{\expval{z}}$. The mean energy $\expval{z}(\tau)$ acts as an intensive thermodynamic control parameter \cite{vespignani1998how}. Its fluctuation thus scales with the system volume \cite{landau1969fluctuations} given by
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\expval{z} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{V}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N^d}}.
\end{equation}
The standard deviation for a linear least squares fit of $W$ values with error free integers on the $x$-axis and constant errors on the $y$-axis is widely known \cite{bevington2003data} to be
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_m = \Delta\expval{z}\sqrt{\frac{12}{W(W^2-1)}} = \sqrt{\frac{12}{N^dW(W^2-1)}}.
\end{equation}
It is thus sensible to choose $\epsilon > 5\sigma_m$. This way, a system is assumed to be unstationary if its fluctuations of $\expval{z}(\tau)$ lie outside the $5\sigma$-interval of the natural fluctuations int $\expval{z}$. The chosen parameters for the check interval and window size are $\Delta\tau_c=1000$ and $W=50$.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{The measurement phase}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

After having passed the transient period and arriving at the critical state, we continually relax and perturb the system according to Algorithm \ref{alg:step}. Every time an avalanche occurs, we track its characteristics $t$, $s$ and $l$, until we have obtained $N_{\text{measure}}$ samples. We estimate the probability distribution of the random variable $X$ via the relative frequency
\begin{equation}
    P(X=x) = \frac{N_x}{N_{\text{measure}}}
\end{equation}
where $N_x$ is the number of samples for which $X=x$. The standard deviation of this estimator is
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_P = \sqrt{\frac{P(x)(1-P(x))}{N}}.
\end{equation}
The conditional expectation values are measured via
\begin{align}
    \bE [X\mid Y=y] &= \sum_x xP(X=x\mid Y=y) \nonumber \\
    &\approx \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N X_i\one_{\{Y_i=y\}}}{N_y},
\end{align}
where $\one$ is the indicator function. Their standard deviation can be estimated as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_\bE = \sqrt{\frac{\f{Var}(X\mid Y=y)}{N_y}}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
    \f{Var}(X\mid Y=y) = \frac{1}{N_y-1}\sum_{i=1}^N (X_i-\bar{X}_y)^2\one_{\{Y_i=y\}}.
\end{equation}
To justify this measurement approach, we need to assume avalanche variables $X_i$ to be identically and independently distributed \cite{pruessner2012numerical}.

\subsection{Calculating scaling exponents}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

After estimating the relevant probability density functions and expectation values from our measurements, we plot them on a log-log scale. Since
\begin{align}
    && F(x) &= C\cdot x^A \nonumber \\
    \iff&& \log_{10}(F(x)) &= A\cdot\log_{10}(x) + C,
\end{align}
we can obtain the scaling exponent (with uncertainty) as the slope of a weighted linear least squares fit through the logarithmic data. We need to take into account the uncertainties of the input data
\begin{align}
    \sigma_{\log_{10}P} &= \frac{1}{10P}\sigma_P \\
\intertext{and}
    \sigma_{\log_{10}\bE} &= \frac{1}{10\bE}\sigma_\bE.
\end{align}
In an infinite lattice, we would expect the probability densities to strictly obey a power law. However due to the constraints of a finite lattice, we deviate from a power law correspondence at very low and high values of the variable $x$. As a finite sized lattice cannot support avalanches of arbitrary sizes, the probability of measuring an avalanche size drops exponentially after a certain threshold, breaking the power law for large values. At very small scales, the self similarity and thus continuous scale-invariance of the system is not yet fulfilled, which also causes deviations from the power law \cite{pruessner2012scaling}. Hence, in order to measure the scaling exponents as accurately as possible, one needs to limit the input data for the linear fit toward a linear scaling region.

To determine a suitable fitting window in a reproducible way, a rolling-window procedure is employed. A linear regression is performed on each window, and for the $i$-th window the slope $m_i$, its standard error $\Delta m_i$, and the coefficient of determination $r_i^2$ are recorded. Adjacent windows are then compared by forming the slope difference $d_i$ and its propagated uncertainty $\Delta d_i$,
\begin{equation}
    d_i = \abs{m_i-m_{i+1}}, \qquad
    \Delta d_i = \sqrt{(\Delta m_i)^2+(\Delta m_{i+1})^2}.
\end{equation}
A window pair is classified as stable if the slope variation is statistically compatible with zero, $d_i < k\cdot\Delta d_i$, and if both fits satisfy $r_i^2 \ge r_{\mathrm{thresh}}$ and $r_{i+1}^2 \ge r_{\mathrm{thresh}}$, where $k$ is a chosen deviation factor and $r_{\mathrm{thresh}}$ is a minimum fit-quality threshold. The final fitting interval is obtained by selecting the longest contiguous stable region and using its corresponding lower and upper boundaries for the exponent regression. At this point it is important to note that the procedure described above is heuristic and does not yield a exact or unique scaling windows. The parameters $k$ and $r_{\mathrm{thresh}}$, as well as the window size, are tuned manually and can vary between model configurations.
