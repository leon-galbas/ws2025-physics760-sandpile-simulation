In the following, we discuss our results and the conclusions that can be drawn from them, comparing them to our main source of Christensen et al. \cite{christensen1991dynamical}.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Burn-in dynamics and monitoring}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Models A and B behave differently because they introduce energy differently. Model A uses nonconservative driving, so it increases the total energy directly and the system reaches the stationary energy density relatively quickly. Model B uses a conservative perturbation. In the bulk it keeps the total energy roughly constant and mainly redistributes what is already there. Starting from an empty lattice, this makes model B take much longer to build up to the stationary level than model A.

A reasonable explanation is that, with our boundary conditions, conservative perturbations only create a net energy increase through boundary effects. The conservative update subtracts energy from shifted neighbour sites. At the boundaries some of these sites are clamped (especially at the lower boundary), so this subtraction can be partly blocked. This means the perturbation can effectively add energy only when it interacts with the edges, while perturbations in the bulk are close to energy-neutral. If most of the net input comes from the edges, then the build-up from $z_0=0$ is naturally slower and more sensitive to how the boundaries are implemented.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Reducing burn-in by changing the initial condition}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The long burn-in in model B is mainly due to how slowly the system fills up when starting from $z_0=0$. A direct way to shorten it is to start closer to the stationary energy density. This motivates the alternative initial conditions: random initialization $z_0\sim U(0,z_c)$ and maximally stable initialization $z_0=z_c$. Both preload the lattice with energy, so fewer driving steps are needed before $\langle z\rangle(\tau)$ stops drifting.

These initial conditions also reduce the time spent in a clearly nonstationary regime, where avalanches are not representative of the steady state. The benefit is largest for conservative driving, where the fill-up stage is slowest. For cases that equilibrate quickly, the burn-in time still cannot drop below the built-in minimum $T_{\min}$ set by the stationarity check procedure.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Scaling Behavior and Avalanche Statistics}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Once the burn-in phase concludes, the system arrives at a self-organized critical state where the average energy $\langle z \rangle(\tau)$ fluctuates stably around a temporal mean $\overline{\langle z \rangle}$. The probability distributions for the measured avalanche observables, lifetime $t$, size $s$, and linear size $l$, exhibit approximate power-law behavior within an intermediate scaling region. The emergence of power laws directly indicates the absence of an intrinsic length or time scale within the system. In the self-organized critical state, spatial correlations extend across all length scales of the lattice. Consequently, a single microscopic perturbation can trigger a relaxation event of any size, ranging from a single local rearrangement to a massive, system-spanning avalanche. This continuous scale invariance confirms that the stationary regime acts as a true critical attractor. The scaling exponents $\alpha$, $\tau$, and $\lambda$ govern these respective distributions and mathematically formalize this scale-free behavior.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Dimensionality, Boundary Conditions, and Driving Mechanisms}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The measured scaling exponents depend heavily on how the system is driven[cite: 10]. In two dimensions ($d=2$), the conservative drive (Configuration B) results in steeper probability distributions, with $\alpha \approx 2.280$ and $\tau \approx 2.414$. In contrast, the nonconservative drive (Configuration A) produces lower values of $\alpha \approx 2.018$ and $\tau \approx 2.058$. This difference occurs because the nonconservative drive acts as an external energy source, simply adding a net unit of energy at a random location. The conservative drive, however, strictly redistributes existing energy locally by shifting $d$ units to the chosen center and taking one unit from each neighbor. Because these fundamental interaction rules are so different, they change the underlying avalanche behavior and place the systems into completely different universality classes. On the other hand, the boundary conditions do not have as much impact. If the nonconservative drive is maintained while changing from open to closed boundaries (comparing Configurations A and C), the scaling exponents remain very similar. Since the local relaxation rules dictate the scale-free nature and energy is injected uniformly, the exact geometry of how energy leaves the edges does not significantly alter the overall scaling behavior.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Literature Comparison and Mean-Field Limit}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The measured scaling exponents across the simulated configurations are broadly consistent with the foundational numerical estimates established by Christensen, Fogedby, and Jensen \cite{christensen1991dynamical}. The largest deviations from expected theoretical values, coupled with the highest measurement uncertainties, manifest in four dimensions ($d=4$). The total number of lattice sites scales with the volume $|R| = N^{d}$. To maintain computational feasibility, the linear size of the $d=4$ lattices is restricted to $N=20$. Because the maximum physical extent of an avalanche is strictly bounded by the linear system size $N$, the intermediate scaling region is severely compressed. This narrow window makes it exceedingly difficult to decouple the theoretical power-law behavior from the lower lattice cutoff and the upper exponential finite-size cutoff. Consequently, the heuristic fitting procedure struggles to find a stable linear region, leading to higher systematic errors and significant deviations from the values predicted by mean-field theory.
